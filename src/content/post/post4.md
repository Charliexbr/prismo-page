**I recommend this show.**

It's an animated show created by Adventure Time's Pendleton Ward and the podcaster Duncan Trussell. It follows a space podcaster who uses a multiverse simulator to travel to worlds on the brink of destruction (usually due to avoidable operator error) where he interviews the inhabitants.

![the midnight gospel](https://occ-0-2299-116.1.nflxso.net/dnm/api/v6/E8vDc_W8CLv7-yMQu8KMEC7Rrr8/AAAABVCC2sd1zlrjeY7rFVUYTlKyhG_8Ri90qm-higqS15EXCAxfaa49dBCXiQixUCMW9ZFylh9cr-IX2IBgY--FdAAi3t9wTjtDjWmn.jpg?r=138)

On the surface, The Midnight Gospel is a spiritual, surrealist journey through time and space itself. The show explores various topics for Clancy to propose throughout his interviews; these topics include, but are not limited to, recreational drug use, meditation, grief, and forgiveness. The show’s flow is hardly disrupted by the psuedo-psychedelic animation style. Trussel’s conversational style pairs well with Ward’s affinity for story-driven attitude towards animation. While it may be easy for the viewer to lose themselves in Ward’s free-flowing animation style or Trussel’s engaging interviews, I’m here to argue that the heart of The Midnight Gospel goes beyond the trippy visuals; rather, The Midnight Gospel aims to highlight the perils of self-imposed emotional neglect via feigned “wokeness” or, in Clancy’s mindset, “enlightenment”.
